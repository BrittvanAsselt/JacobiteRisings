The Battle of Culloden, April 16 1746

a Doomed Battle

The battle lasted less than an hour.
The Jacobites had already lost a significant amount of men to desertion and death before the battle began. They started the battle with only 5000 hungry and exhausted troops, who had to fight against 7000 to 9000 well-rested, well-equipped, and well-supplied men. Likely between 1500 and 2000 Jacobites were killed or wounded, whereas on the English government’s side, these numbers were only estimated to be around 300. After the battle, wounded Jacobite were killed where they lay and stayed unburied. Others were stripped and left to die. Charles escaped to France.

The Aftermath and Now

In the weeks after, Government forces searched for rebels, confiscated cattle and burned settlements. Men were killed for wearing Highland dress (tartan and kilts), women were raped and killed and children were slaughtered. The leader of the government forces, the Duke of Cumberland, was nicknamed Butcher Cumberland. The clan system (from Gaelic clann, ‘family/children’) was dismantled and Highland dress was outlawed.
Charles was unable to secure support for another attempt in his lifetime, and died in Rome in 1788, reportedly a bitter and disappointed man. Jacobitism as a political force declined after this battle, but remained as a sentiment. The Jacobite Risings, especially the Rising of ’45, have been highly romanticized throughout time. ‘’The king over the water’’ gained a sentimental appeal, especially in the Scottish Highlands, and many Jacobite songs were written, with one such song being the soundtrack of the TV-show Outlander.
